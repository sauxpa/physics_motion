Central force motion

Of Newton and Coulomb

Newton's law of universal gravitation posits that two punctal objects $P_0$ and $P_1$ of mass $m_0$ and $M_1$ are subject to a central attractive force exerted on each other: $$ \mathbf{F}_{0\rightarrow 1} = -\frac{m_0 m_1 G}{\lVert \mathbf{r} \rVert^2} \frac{\mathbf{r}}{\lVert \mathbf{r}\rVert} $$ where $G$ is the universal gravitational constant and $\mathbf{r}=\overrightarrow{P_0 P_1}$. This governs the motion of stars and planets (in the non-relativistic limit).

Similarly, Coulomb's law states that a similar interaction applies if the punctual objects bear electrical charges $q_0$ and $q_1$ instead of a mass, which is called electrostatic force: $$ \mathbf{F}_{0\rightarrow 1} = \frac{q_0 q_1 k}{\lVert \mathbf{r} \rVert^2} \frac{\mathbf{r}}{\lVert \mathbf{r}\rVert} $$ where $k$ is the Coulomb's constant.

To summarize, two of the fundamental forces in physics share the same property: their magnitude is invertly proportional to the squre of the distance between the two objects.

What if...

... the laws of physics were different? Instead of the square, what if the fundamental forces relies on another exponent $\alpha$? What if their magnitude were decreasing with distance instead ($\alpha$>0)? Constant ($\alpha=0$)?
